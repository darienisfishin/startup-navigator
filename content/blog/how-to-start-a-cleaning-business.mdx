Cleaning businesses are one of the most reliable ways to build a profitable company from scratch with minimal startup capital. The residential cleaning industry alone exceeds $10 billion in the U.S., and commercial cleaning adds tens of billions more. The barriers to entry are low, demand is consistent, and it's a business you can start part-time and grow into a full operation.

But "low barriers to entry" also means high competition. The operators who win are the ones who understand how to start a cleaning business strategically — not just show up with a mop and hope for referrals.

## Residential vs. Commercial: Choose Your Lane

Before anything else, decide which market you're targeting. Most new cleaning businesses start with one and add the other later.

**Residential cleaning** (houses, apartments, condos) offers faster sales cycles, lower equipment requirements, and word-of-mouth growth. Clients are easier to find and easier to please. The downside: higher churn, scheduling complexity, and more price-sensitive customers.

**Commercial cleaning** (offices, retail, medical facilities) involves longer sales cycles and more competitive bidding, but contracts are larger, more predictable, and often month-to-month or annual. A single commercial contract can be worth $30,000–$100,000+ per year.

Many successful operators start residential to build cash flow and credibility, then pursue commercial contracts as they grow.

## Startup Costs: What You'll Actually Spend

Cleaning is one of the most affordable businesses to launch:

- **Cleaning supplies and chemicals:** $200–$500 to start
- **Equipment (mops, vacuums, microfiber cloths, caddies):** $300–$1,000
- **Business license and LLC formation:** $50–$500
- **General liability insurance:** $400–$800/year (non-negotiable)
- **Website and basic marketing:** $200–$500
- **Branded uniforms or shirts:** $100–$300
- **Transportation (if you need a vehicle):** varies

You can realistically launch a residential cleaning business for **$1,000–$3,000**. Commercial cleaning that requires specialized equipment (floor buffers, carpet extractors, pressure washers) will run higher — $5,000–$20,000.

## Licenses, Insurance, and Legal Setup

**Form an LLC.** As a cleaning professional, you're entering people's homes and businesses. If something gets damaged or stolen, a sole proprietorship leaves your personal assets exposed. An LLC costs $50–$200 to form and is worth every dollar.

**General liability insurance** is essential. Most residential clients will ask if you're insured before booking. Commercial clients may require $1M–$2M in coverage. Expect to pay $400–$1,200/year for a solid policy.

**Bonding.** A surety bond protects clients against employee theft. Many clients — especially those hiring for recurring service — will ask if you're bonded. It typically costs $100–$200/year.

**Workers' comp insurance** becomes required in most states as soon as you hire your first employee. Budget for it early.

## Pricing Your Services for Profit

Underpricing is the #1 reason cleaning businesses fail. Many new operators charge $15–$20/hour because they're nervous about losing bids — and then wonder why they can't make ends meet after expenses.

**Sustainable pricing:**
- **Residential cleaning:** $25–$50/hour per cleaner, or flat rates of $100–$300 per home depending on size
- **Deep cleans and move-in/move-out:** $200–$500+ (premium pricing for premium effort)
- **Commercial:** Typically bid per square foot ($0.05–$0.20/sq ft for routine cleaning)

Calculate your costs first: supplies, insurance, transportation, your time, and any employee wages. Then price above that. If you're not making at least $35–$50/hour net after expenses, raise your rates.

## Finding Your First Clients

**Nextdoor and Facebook Groups.** These are gold for residential cleaning. Post a professional introduction, offer a discounted first clean for reviews, and let word of mouth do the rest.

**Google Business Profile.** Set this up on day one. A cleaned-up profile with a few reviews will generate inbound leads for years.

**Direct outreach for commercial.** Identify target businesses (small offices, dental practices, gyms) and send a professional email or stop in to introduce yourself. Offer a free walkthrough and quote.

**Referral incentives.** Give existing clients a discount or gift card for every new client they refer. Residential clients who love you will happily send you their neighbors.

## Scaling Beyond Yourself

The real money in cleaning is in building a team. Once you're at capacity alone ($5,000–$8,000/month in revenue), hire and train a second cleaner, raise your rates slightly to absorb the management overhead, and use the freed-up time to sell more accounts.

Systematize everything: cleaning checklists, client onboarding, quality control inspections. The clients who stick around for years are the ones who get consistent results every visit.
