The food truck industry generates over $1.2 billion in revenue annually in the United States, and it's one of the most accessible entry points into the restaurant business. Lower overhead than a brick-and-mortar, built-in mobility, and the freedom to test different markets make it an attractive model — but knowing how to start a food truck business correctly is what separates thriving operators from the ones who fold within a year.

This guide covers everything: concept development, realistic startup costs, permits you'll actually need, and the operational details that most guides skip.

## What Is a Food Truck Business (and Is It Right for You)?

A food truck is a mobile food service operation that prepares and sells food from a vehicle. The model ranges from simple hot dog carts to fully outfitted trucks serving gourmet cuisine. Before diving in, be honest with yourself about what you're signing up for: long days, unpredictable weather, intense competition at popular spots, and the physical demands of working in a small, hot space.

That said, the upside is real. You can pivot your concept, test new markets without signing a lease, and build a loyal following that follows you wherever you go.

## Startup Costs: What to Actually Budget

Food truck startup costs vary widely based on whether you buy new, used, or converted:

- **New custom-built truck:** $75,000–$200,000
- **Used truck (needs refurbishment):** $20,000–$60,000
- **Converted vehicle:** $15,000–$40,000
- **Commissary kitchen fees:** $400–$1,200/month
- **Permits and licenses:** $1,000–$5,000 (varies heavily by city)
- **Initial food inventory:** $1,000–$3,000
- **Generator (if not built in):** $2,000–$8,000
- **POS system and equipment:** $500–$2,000
- **Branding, wraps, and marketing:** $2,000–$6,000
- **Working capital (first 3 months):** $10,000–$20,000

Plan for a total launch budget of **$50,000–$100,000** for a professionally outfitted used truck with working capital. Going lower is possible, but cutting corners on the truck itself is a frequent source of costly breakdowns at the worst possible times.

## Licenses and Permits You'll Need

This is where food truck operators get tripped up more than anywhere else. Requirements vary dramatically by city and state.

**Universal requirements:**
- Business license (LLC strongly recommended for liability protection)
- Food handler's certification for you and all employees
- Food truck permit or mobile food vendor license from your city
- Health department inspection and permit
- Fire safety inspection (especially for trucks with open flames)
- Sales tax permit from your state revenue department

**Location-specific requirements:**
- Many cities require a separate permit for each location you operate in
- Some municipalities limit how close food trucks can operate to brick-and-mortar restaurants
- Special event permits if you're serving at festivals or private events

**Pro tip:** Call your city's planning or business licensing office before you buy a truck. Some cities have moratoriums on new permits or caps on truck density in popular areas.

## The Commissary Kitchen Requirement

Most cities require food trucks to operate out of a licensed commissary kitchen — a commercial kitchen where you prep food, store supplies, and clean your equipment each day. This is not optional in most jurisdictions.

Commissary fees run $400–$1,200/month depending on how much time you need. Many commissaries also offer storage for your truck overnight, which matters if you don't have a place to park it safely.

## Choosing Your Concept and Location Strategy

**Pick a focused concept.** The most successful food trucks have a clear identity: Korean BBQ tacos, gourmet grilled cheese, acai bowls, authentic birria. Trying to serve everything means excelling at nothing.

**Scout high-traffic locations.** Office parks during lunch, breweries in the evening, weekend farmers markets, and food truck parks are all proven venues. Study where trucks are already successful in your city — then look for gaps.

**Diversify your revenue streams.** The most profitable food trucks don't just park on the street. They also book private catering for corporate events, weddings, and parties, which offers predictable revenue and higher margins.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Underestimating permit timelines.** Health department inspections and city permits can take 4–12 weeks. Many first-time operators plan to launch in 60 days and find themselves waiting 4 months.

**Ignoring vehicle maintenance.** A truck breakdown on a Saturday at a busy event can wipe out weeks of profit. Budget at least $300–$500/month for maintenance and build relationships with a mechanic who knows commercial vehicles.

**Poor location strategy.** Parking somewhere because it's cheap rather than because customers are there is a recipe for slow days. Track your sales by location obsessively and double down on what works.

**Underpricing.** Food truck operators frequently price to compete with fast food, forgetting that their cost structure (commissary, permits, fuel, vehicle) is substantial. Price for your actual costs plus a real profit margin.
